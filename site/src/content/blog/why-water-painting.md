There is a practice, centuries old, of painting with water on stone.

You dip a brush, trace characters or shapes onto a flat rock, and watch them appear. Then, slowly, they vanish. The sun takes them. The air takes them. And you begin again.

It is called *shuǐ shū* in Chinese — literally, water writing. Elderly practitioners gather in parks in Beijing and Shanghai, wielding brushes the size of brooms, writing classical poems on sidewalks that disappear before anyone has fully read them.

I came across a video of this a few years ago and I couldn't stop thinking about it.

## What is it actually doing?

On the surface, it is just... painting. But the impermanence changes everything. When you know your work will disappear, you stop trying to make it good. You stop protecting it. You just paint.

That shift — from *making something* to *doing something* — is what I kept returning to. Most creative tools are designed to help you produce. A better output. A saved file. Something to show.

Water painting produces nothing. And somehow, that feels like the point.

## The question I couldn't answer

For a while I kept asking: why does it feel so freeing?

I think it is because the usual burden of creative work — the self-judgment, the comparison, the fear of being seen and found lacking — has nowhere to attach. The painting is already gone. There is nothing to critique.

What's left is just the sensation of the brush. The feeling of your hand moving. The brief appearance and the slow fade. Over and over.

That is the thing I wanted to make.

## The game that grew from it

Serenity Sketch started as a single experiment: can I recreate that sensation on an iPad? The physics of water spreading on textured stone, the way it catches light for a moment, the quiet of watching it go.

It took a long time to get it to feel right. Not realistic — *felt* right. The kind of right that makes you slow down without realising you have.

From that, everything else grew: the Guide, the Paths, the Journey of Letting Go. But the water painting came first. It is still, in some ways, the whole game.
